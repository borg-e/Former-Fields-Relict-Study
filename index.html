Former Fields Relicts: European synthesis Map

Reconstructing the history of land use, and thereby assessing the ecological legacies associated with past practices, is a key prerequisite for effective environmental governance (Ellis, 2021). Former agricultural landforms —often organized within former field systems (FFS)— are widely preserved across Europe. Among the most common micro-topographies described in the literature are lynchets, terraces, ridge-and-furrow systems, stone walls and headland ridges. LiDAR has enabled numerous regional studies on landscape history and heritage, including vast areas now covered by forest. On the other hand, geoarchaeological surveys have provided an initial, though still incomplete, chronological framework. This study encompasses the temperate transition zone where oceanic and continental influences met. The scope also aims to follow the historical diffusion of the plow, tracing its path from its Central European origins to Alsace and Lorraine (Haudricourt and Delamarre, 1988). Furthermore, it extends to the North-Western French openfields, spanning from the Paris Basin to the Beauce region, to ensure a comprehensive analysis of these traditional agricultural landscapes.
